BALTIMORE AIR QUALITY PRESENTATION SCRIPT
=========================================

QUICK FAQ - TERMS USED IN THIS DECK
-----------------------------------

Wheelabrator: the trash-burning incinerator in South Baltimore.
Officially WIN Waste Innovations. Baltimore's biggest single air
pollution source by permitted emissions.

I-95: the interstate highway that runs right past Wheelabrator.
The other major pollution source in the same corner of the city.

PM2.5: fine particulate matter. Tiny particles 2.5 micrometers or
smaller, small enough to get deep into the lungs. Measured in
micrograms per cubic meter of air (ug/m3). This is the pollutant
I focused on.

Monitor: an EPA air quality station that reports a daily PM2.5
reading. I used four of them in the Baltimore area.

Monitor-day: one day of readings from one monitor. So four
monitors times roughly 2,000 days is the total amount of evidence
I am working with.

Wheelabrator-only day: a day at a monitor when the wind was
blowing from the incinerator toward that monitor (inside a 45
degree cone on either side of the straight line between them) for
more than half the hours of the day, AND the wind was NOT also
doing that for I-95.

I-95-only day: same idea, but wind pointed from the highway
toward the monitor and not also from Wheelabrator.

Both day: a day when the wind qualified as pointed from BOTH
Wheelabrator and I-95 at that monitor. Happens when the two
sources are close to the same direction from that monitor.

Neither day: a day when the wind qualified as pointed from
NEITHER. Either the wind was from a totally different direction,
or the direction was unstable across the day.

Downwind: wind is blowing FROM the source TOWARD you. If the wind
is from the southwest, the monitor to the northeast is downwind.

Wind rose: a polar chart. Each spoke points in a compass
direction, and the length of the spoke shows how often wind comes
from that direction. Long spoke on the southwest means wind is
often coming from the southwest.

Southwesterly wind: wind blowing from the southwest. Relevant
here because it is the same wind that carries Ohio Valley and
mid-Atlantic regional pollution into Baltimore. Most Wheelabrator
days at these monitors were also southwesterly days, which is the
main confounder in this analysis.

Regional transport: pollution that was produced somewhere else
(like Ohio, Pennsylvania, or West Virginia) and blew into
Baltimore on the wind. Not local to the city.

Delta: the percent difference between the Wheelabrator-only
average and the I-95-only average at a given monitor. Positive
delta means Wheelabrator days were dirtier.

Angular offset: the number of degrees between the direction to
Wheelabrator and the direction to the nearest point on I-95, from
a given monitor's point of view. Small offset means the method
cannot really tell the two sources apart at that monitor.


Note on the charts: every chart in this deck is interactive. You
can click-and-drag to zoom in on any chart, and double-click
anywhere inside it to reset back to the full view. A small
toolbar also appears in the top-right corner of each chart with a
"home" button for the same thing.


SLIDE 1 - DIRECTIONAL ANALYSIS OF WHEELABRATOR AIR POLLUTION
IN BALTIMORE
I looked at whether the air gets dirtier in Baltimore
neighborhoods when the wind is blowing from the Wheelabrator
incinerator, compared to when the wind is blowing from the I-95
highway. Both are big pollution sources sitting right next to
each other in South Baltimore, and most of the city is downwind
of them on a pretty regular basis.

How to read this slide: the map on the right shows the Baltimore
area with Wheelabrator and I-95 marked, along with the
neighborhoods I grouped for comparison. You can pan and zoom the
map. The chips across the top are the headline numbers for the
whole study: how many monitors I used, how many hours of wind
data, and so on.


SLIDE 2 - RESEARCH QUESTION, OBJECTIVE, AND OUTLINE
The question was simple: is Wheelabrator making the air worse on
top of what I-95 is already doing? To answer it I sorted every
day at every monitor into one of four buckets based on where the
wind was coming from that day. Wind from Wheelabrator. Wind from
the highway. Wind from both. Wind from neither. Then I compared
the air quality numbers across those buckets. Quick note on what
those buckets actually mean. For each monitor I drew a straight
line to the incinerator. If the wind was blowing from that
direction, inside a 45 degree cone on either side of that line,
for more than half the hours of the day, that day counts as a
Wheelabrator-wind day at that monitor. Same rule for the highway,
using the closest point on I-95 to that monitor. So when I say
"wind from Wheelabrator," I mean the wind was actually pointed
from the incinerator toward that specific neighborhood for most
of the day. Not a guess, not a general direction, an actual line
from the source to the monitor.

How to read this slide: no charts here, just the question and a
photo of the Wheelabrator incinerator so you know what we are
actually talking about. The card on the right is an outline of
how the rest of the slides flow, context first, then numbers,
then the stress-test, then what I think it means.


SLIDE 3 - STUDY AREA AND EXPOSURE GROUPS
I picked three kinds of neighborhoods. Six sit next to both
Wheelabrator and I-95. Six more sit along I-95 but far enough
from the incinerator that it should not reach them. Four sit away
from both. That third group is basically a sanity check. The
middle group is the important one, because it tells me what
highway pollution alone looks like, without the incinerator in
the mix.

How to read this slide: the map is interactive. Wheelabrator is
marked with an icon, I-95 runs through as a line, and the
neighborhoods are color-coded by group: near both sources, I-95
only, and the control group. Hover or click on a point for the
neighborhood name.


SLIDE 4 - DATASETS AND DIRECTIONAL METHOD
I used three sources. Wheelabrator's reported emissions, which
tell me what the smokestack is putting out. Six years of hourly
wind data from BWI airport, which tells me where that smoke is
going. And daily PM2.5 readings from EPA air monitors in the
Baltimore area, which tell me what people are actually breathing.
Putting those three together is what lets me say "on this day at
this monitor, the wind was carrying Wheelabrator plume toward
this neighborhood."

How to read this slide: the photo on the right is an example of
an EPA air monitor, the kind of station that produced the daily
PM2.5 readings I used. The diagram across the bottom is the
pipeline, left to right: the three data sources flow into the
daily downwind classification, which flows into the source
comparison. One monitor, one day, one bucket.


SLIDE 5 - FACILITY EMISSIONS
Wheelabrator is a serious polluter by the numbers. In 2017 it
reported 1,101 tons of nitrogen oxides, 308 tons of sulfur
dioxide, 27 tons of fine particulate matter, and 762,000 metric
tons of greenhouse gases. Between 2014 and 2017, total reported
emissions went up a little, not down. Mercury and lead dropped,
but hydrogen fluoride and nickel climbed. None of this proves
anyone's PM2.5 reading came from Wheelabrator, but it does show
the facility is not a small source.

How to read this slide: the bar chart on the left shows emissions
by pollutant. Each pollutant has two bars: blue is 2014, red is
2017. Taller bars mean more pounds or tons per year. Use the
"Major Pollutants" tab for the big pollutants in tons per year,
and the "Trace Pollutants" tab for metals and specialty
pollutants in pounds per year. The stat boxes on the right are
the four headline 2017 numbers.


SLIDE 6 - WIND PATTERNS AND SEASONAL TRANSPORT
Wind is the link between the smokestack and the monitor. Over
40,000 hours of observations show Baltimore gets wind from pretty
much every direction, but west and northwest are the most common,
with a lot of southwesterly wind mixed in. The direction changes
day to day, so the same neighborhood is not always downwind of
the same thing, which is exactly what makes this kind of analysis
work. Southwesterly wind is going to come back in a couple of
slides because it matters more than I originally thought.

How to read this slide: two charts. The polar bar chart on the
left is a simple version: each spoke points in a compass
direction, N at top, E at right, and the longer the spoke, the
more often wind comes from that direction. The wind rose on the
right shows the same idea but adds wind speed, colored rings
inside each spoke. Tabs above it let you switch between the
overall rose and each season. Photo below is BWI airport, the one
weather station feeding all of this. Key point for the audience:
a long spoke on the southwest means wind is coming from the
southwest, which means it is blowing toward the northeast, which
is where most of the monitors are. That is why southwesterly wind
matters.


SLIDE 7 - MAIN PM2.5 FINDING
Here is the headline. On days when the wind was blowing from
Wheelabrator but not from I-95, PM2.5 averaged 9.0 micrograms per
cubic meter. On days when it was blowing from I-95 but not from
Wheelabrator, it averaged 6.3. That is a 43 percent gap. When the
wind was coming from both sources at once, the numbers were
almost as high as the Wheelabrator-only days. When the wind was
coming from neither, they dropped. The pattern points in the
direction you would expect if Wheelabrator were adding something
on top of the highway background. But I want to say up front that
a 43 percent gap is the first thing I saw, not the last. I spent
the next few slides pressure-testing it.

How to read this chart: four bars, one per wind bucket. The
height of each bar is the average PM2.5 reading on days in that
bucket, across every monitor. The small text above each bar is
the sample size, how many monitor-days are in that bucket. Red is
Wheelabrator-only, orange is I-95-only, purple is both, gray is
neither. You are reading this bar chart for the ordering: red
above orange, and by how much.


SLIDE 8 - MONITOR-BY-MONITOR WHEELABRATOR SIGNAL
The gap is not the same everywhere. Three of the four monitors
show higher PM2.5 on Wheelabrator days than on highway days.
Oldtown has the biggest gap, 60 percent higher. Lake Montebello
is 42 percent higher. Padonia is 18 percent higher. Essex goes
the other way. There the highway days are 34 percent higher than
the Wheelabrator days. That matters. If the method were clean, I
would expect at least a small positive gap at every monitor. The
fact that Essex flips tells me something about how local or how
consistent the signal really is.

How to read this chart: the horizontal bar chart on the left
shows each monitor's delta, the percent difference between
Wheelabrator-only days and I-95-only days. Red bars going right
mean Wheelabrator days were dirtier. Blue bars going left mean
highway days were dirtier. The table on the right shows the raw
numbers behind each bar: the WB-only mean, the I-95-only mean,
and the delta. You want to see whether all four monitors lean the
same direction. Three do, Essex does not.


SLIDE 9 - SEASONAL PM2.5 PATTERN
The gap also changes with the seasons. In spring, fall, and
winter, Wheelabrator-wind days average around 60 percent higher
than highway-wind days. In summer the gap mostly disappears,
because summer PM2.5 is high everywhere and it drowns out the
source-specific difference. That is useful information on its
own. It also hints that some of what I am seeing might not be
Wheelabrator at all, because summer is also when regional
pollution from outside Baltimore peaks.

How to read this chart: the line chart has one line per wind
bucket across the four seasons. Follow the red line, Wheelabrator
only, against the orange line, I-95 only. In winter, spring, and
fall the red line sits well above the orange line, that is the
gap I am talking about. In summer the lines bunch together, that
is the gap collapsing. You can also eyeball what summer does to
the overall pollution level: every line climbs, which means
summer is just dirty across the board.


SLIDE 10 - MONITOR EVIDENCE DETAIL
This is the deeper dive. For each monitor I have a pollution
rose, a side-by-side comparison of the four wind buckets, a time
series of daily readings, and a seasonal view. The story is the
same at Oldtown and Lake Montebello: higher readings on the
Wheelabrator-wind days. Essex behaves differently. Padonia is
somewhere in the middle. Rather than walk through each chart, I
want you to know they are here and that none of them contradicted
the headline number.

How to read this slide: pick a monitor from the dropdown on the
left. Then pick an "Evidence view" from the four buttons:
- Pollution Rose: a compass chart where the petals show PM2.5 by
  wind direction. Long red petal coming from the Wheelabrator
  direction means dirtier air on winds from that source.
- Directional: bar chart of the four wind buckets for just this
  monitor, same idea as Slide 7 but one monitor at a time.
- Time Series: daily PM2.5 over time, color-coded by wind bucket.
  Useful for seeing whether the high readings cluster on specific
  days or spread evenly.
- Seasonal: the Slide 9 chart but for this monitor alone.
The text card underneath the dropdown tells you what I read into
that specific monitor.


SLIDE 11 - DIRECTIONAL OVERLAP AND REGIONAL TRANSPORT
This is the slide that changed how I read my own results. Two
things jumped out when I pressure-tested the 43 percent gap.
First, at several monitors, the direction "toward Wheelabrator"
and the direction "toward I-95" are basically the same direction,
because the incinerator and the highway are right next to each
other. So the Wheelabrator-wind days and the highway-wind days
are not always different weather. Second, most of the
Wheelabrator-wind days happen on southwesterly wind, and
southwesterly wind is also what brings dirty air into Baltimore
from the Ohio Valley and the rest of the mid-Atlantic. Roughly
seven out of ten Wheelabrator-wind days were southwesterly. Only
about four out of ten highway-wind days were. That means a chunk
of the headline gap is probably regional pollution riding the
same wind as the incinerator plume. I do not know how big that
chunk is without more work. But I cannot call the whole 43
percent local Wheelabrator impact.

How to read this chart: two small bar charts side by side, one
row per monitor. The left panel shows the angular offset, how
many degrees apart the direction to Wheelabrator and the
direction to I-95 are, from that monitor's point of view. Bars to
the right of the dashed red line at 45 degrees mean the two
directions are distinct enough for the method to tell them apart.
Bars to the left of that line mean the two categories are
overlapping, the monitor cannot really separate them. The right
panel shows the southwesterly share, what percent of Wheelabrator
days at that monitor were on southwesterly wind. High bars mean
most of that monitor's WB-only days are also regional-transport
days. The table below gives you the raw numbers.


SLIDE 12 - CONCLUSIONS
The honest version of my conclusion: the air is worse in
Baltimore on Wheelabrator-wind days than on highway-only days.
That much is real. Whether Wheelabrator is the whole reason, a
big reason, or a small reason on top of regional pollution, this
method cannot say. Three of four monitors lean the way you would
expect if the incinerator is contributing. One monitor does not.
The facility emits a lot of stuff by the numbers, it sits next to
neighborhoods that already live with highway exhaust, and wind
carries its plume into the rest of the city regularly. That is
enough to justify more monitoring. It is not enough, on its own,
to say how much damage the facility is specifically doing.

How to read this slide: two boxes side by side. The green box on
the left is what I can defend from the data, the part I would
stand behind. The red box on the right is what I did not prove,
the weaknesses and the part I am not claiming. Read both before
walking away with a conclusion.


SLIDE 13 - LIMITATIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
A few things I could not fix. I could not fully separate the
incinerator from regional pollution, because they ride the same
wind. I used one wind station at BWI for the whole metro area,
even though wind can be different a few miles away. I used daily
averages, which miss short spikes. And there are no EPA monitors
in Westport, Cherry Hill, or Curtis Bay, which are the
neighborhoods right up against the facility. If I were continuing
this, the next moves would be fence-line monitors in those
neighborhoods, subtracting a regional background from the numbers
before blaming Wheelabrator for the rest, and repeating the same
directional approach for sulfur dioxide and nitrogen dioxide
because those are more unique to local sources than PM2.5 is.

How to read this slide: two columns of bullets, no charts. Left
column is the weaknesses, right column is the next moves.
