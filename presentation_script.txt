BALTIMORE AIR QUALITY PRESENTATION SCRIPT
=========================================

SLIDE 1 - DIRECTIONAL ANALYSIS OF WHEELABRATOR AIR POLLUTION
IN BALTIMORE
I examined whether PM2.5 was higher when Baltimore-area monitors
were downwind of Wheelabrator Baltimore than when they were
downwind of the I-95 corridor alone. To answer that question, I
combined EPA emissions data, EPA AQS monitor data, and hourly
wind observations from BWI. My goal was to separate the
incinerator signal from the broader highway background instead of
treating all urban pollution as the same, while being honest
about what a wind-based screening method can and cannot prove.


SLIDE 2 - RESEARCH QUESTION, OBJECTIVE, AND OUTLINE
My research question was straightforward: was ambient PM2.5
higher when monitors were downwind of Wheelabrator than when they
were downwind of I-95 alone? My objective was to use a
GIS-informed directional method to compare Wheelabrator-only
days, I-95-only days, both-source days, and neither-source days,
and then to stress-test that contrast against the main
confounders. I moved from source context, to study design and
method, to the PM2.5 results, to a directional confound check,
and then to my conclusions and recommendations.


SLIDE 3 - STUDY AREA AND EXPOSURE GROUPS
I divided the study area into three groups. Six neighborhoods
were near both Wheelabrator and I-95, six neighborhoods were in
the I-95 corridor only, and four neighborhoods acted as control
areas. That design mattered because I treated the I-95-only
communities as the transportation baseline. Instead of asking
whether the incinerator area was simply more polluted than
distant places, I asked whether it was more polluted than other
communities exposed to highway traffic but not to Wheelabrator.
The geographic grouping is the framing, while the numerical
analysis used the four AQS PM2.5 monitors that happened to sit
inside the study area.


SLIDE 4 - DATASETS AND DIRECTIONAL METHOD
I compiled Wheelabrator emissions from EPA NEI and greenhouse gas
reporting data. I used hourly wind observations from BWI between
2019 and 2024 to classify which source each monitor was downwind
of on each day. For I-95 I treated the highway as a line source
and took the bearing to the closest point on the corridor
polyline, rather than the nearest single vertex, which avoids
biasing the bearing toward whichever waypoint I happened to
sample. I also deduplicated the AQS PM2.5 data to one daily value
per monitor and date before merging it with the wind record. That
gave me a daily comparison across four source categories:
Wheelabrator only, I-95 only, both, and neither.


SLIDE 5 - FACILITY EMISSIONS
The emissions inventory showed that Wheelabrator was a major
stationary source by mass. In 2017 it reported about 1,101 tons
of NOx, 308 tons of SO2, and 27.3 tons of PM2.5, along with
762,683 metric tons of CO2 equivalent. Total reported emissions
increased from 3,159,929 pounds in 2014 to 3,248,587 pounds in
2017, or about 2.8 percent. NOx remained the largest reported
pollutant by mass, while PM and PM2.5 both increased. Trace
pollutants changed in mixed directions, with mercury and lead
declining while hydrogen fluoride and nickel increased. I used
this inventory to establish that the facility is a meaningful
source by mass, not to attribute any specific PM2.5 reading to
it.


SLIDE 6 - WIND PATTERNS AND SEASONAL TRANSPORT
Wind direction was what connected the emissions inventory to
neighborhood exposure. Across more than 40,000 hourly
observations from 2019 through 2024, I found that westerly and
northwesterly winds were common, with substantial contributions
from easterly and southwesterly sectors as well. That mattered
because downwind status changed from day to day. I did not assume
that the same communities were exposed all the time. Instead, I
let the wind determine when each monitor was more likely to
capture emissions from Wheelabrator or from I-95. Southwesterly
winds are also the classic mid-Atlantic regional PM2.5 transport
pattern, which becomes important in the confound check later.


SLIDE 7 - MAIN PM2.5 FINDING
This was the core result of the project. Across all monitor-days
in the deduplicated PM2.5 dataset, I found that Wheelabrator-only
days averaged 9.00 micrograms per cubic meter, while I-95-only
days averaged 6.31 micrograms per cubic meter, a 42.6 percent
difference. Days downwind of both sources averaged 8.72
micrograms per cubic meter, compared with 7.43 micrograms per
cubic meter on neither days, which was 17.4 percent higher. I
treated this as a directional association that is consistent with
a facility contribution, not as proof of causation, because the
same winds that bring Wheelabrator toward the northern monitors
also bring regional transport into Baltimore.


SLIDE 8 - MONITOR-BY-MONITOR WHEELABRATOR SIGNAL
The monitor-level comparison showed that the pattern was not
identical everywhere, and that variation matters. I found that
three of the four PM2.5 monitors showed higher mean PM2.5 on
Wheelabrator-only days than on I-95-only days. The strongest
positive signal appeared at Oldtown, where the Wheelabrator-only
mean was 60.5 percent higher than the I-95-only mean. Lake
Montebello also showed a strong positive signal at 42.2 percent,
and Padonia showed a smaller positive signal at 17.7 percent.
Essex went the other direction, with I-95-only days 33.9 percent
higher than Wheelabrator-only days. I treated Essex as a real
counter-example: if the method produced a clean Wheelabrator
signal, I would expect at least a small positive delta there as
well. The fact that it reverses is informative about how local
the signal actually is.


SLIDE 9 - SEASONAL PM2.5 PATTERN
The seasonal breakdown showed that the Wheelabrator-versus-I-95
contrast was not equally strong all year. I found that in spring,
fall, and winter combined, Wheelabrator-only days stayed about
61.3 percent above I-95-only days. In summer, PM2.5 rose across
every category, so the contrast between sources became less
distinct. My reading is that the directional signal was clearest
outside the summer season, when the regional PM2.5 background was
lower, and that the summer wash-out is itself consistent with
regional transport because summer is when that regional
background is highest.


SLIDE 10 - MONITOR EVIDENCE DETAIL
At this point I moved into the monitor-specific evidence. I
compared pollution roses, directional comparisons, time series,
and seasonal plots for each monitor. Oldtown and Lake Montebello
showed the clearest central-city pattern on Wheelabrator-leaning
winds, while Essex behaved differently and appeared to be more
strongly shaped by eastern corridor influences. This slide let me
connect the summary statistics to the underlying spatial and
temporal patterns at each site.


SLIDE 11 - DIRECTIONAL OVERLAP AND REGIONAL TRANSPORT
I stress-tested the main finding against two confounders before
reaching my conclusions. First, I checked how close the bearing
from each monitor to Wheelabrator was to the bearing from that
monitor to I-95. For the northern monitors the two bearings sit
within the 45 degree tolerance that I used for the classifier,
which means the Wheelabrator-only and I-95-only categories are
not fully independent wind regimes. Second, I measured what
fraction of Wheelabrator-only monitor-days had mean winds from
the 180 to 270 degree southwesterly quadrant, which is the same
quadrant that carries Ohio Valley and mid-Atlantic regional PM2.5
into Baltimore. A large share of the Wheelabrator-only days fell
into that quadrant, and a noticeably smaller share of the
I-95-only days did. That tells me a real part of the raw 42.6
percent gap is confounded with regional transport, not purely
local facility impact. I do not treat the gap as zero, but I also
do not treat it as clean source attribution.


SLIDE 12 - CONCLUSIONS
My main conclusion was that the directional PM2.5 pattern is
consistent with a facility contribution at several Baltimore-area
monitors, but I cannot separate that contribution from regional
southwesterly transport with this method. I found a positive
Wheelabrator-leaning delta at three of the four PM2.5 monitors,
and days downwind of both sources were higher than days downwind
of neither source. At the same time, I treated Essex as a real
counter-example and I treated the southwesterly-wind confound as
a live alternative explanation. The honest read is that this
screening analysis points toward a facility signal that warrants
follow-up monitoring, not that it settles the attribution
question.


SLIDE 13 - LIMITATIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
I treated this study as a screening analysis, not a source
attribution model. I could not fully separate Wheelabrator from
regional southwesterly transport because both arrive on the same
winds at the northern monitors. I relied on wind data from the
BWI airport station rather than neighborhood-scale meteorology,
and I used daily PM2.5 averages that smoothed short-duration
spikes. I did not control for wind speed, mixing height, or
synoptic weather patterns. The AQS network was also sparse near
the closest fence-line communities. If I had continued this work,
I would have added fence-line monitoring in Westport, Cherry
Hill, and Curtis Bay, subtracted a regional background from an
upwind rural monitor or a chemical-transport-model baseline
before attributing the remaining signal to Wheelabrator, expanded
the directional method to pollutants like SO2 and NO2 where the
local fingerprint is more distinct than PM2.5, and paired this
analysis with local wind fields or dispersion modeling to
strengthen attribution.
